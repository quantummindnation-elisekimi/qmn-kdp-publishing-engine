## Main Character

**Working name:** Wren *(working draft — trademark search required before finalizing)*
**Age:** 5 years old
**Gender presentation:** Gender-neutral (illustrator may interpret as the child reader sees fit; no gender-specific pronouns forced in text)

### Physical Appearance

*(Original descriptions — no comparison to known characters)*

- **Build:** Small for their age, slightly round-faced, compact and cozy-looking
- **Hair:** Short, slightly messy, warm chestnut brown — sticks up a little in the morning
- **Eyes:** Large and dark, expressive — they show everything Wren feels
- **Distinctive feature:** Wren has a small, particular way of tilting their head to one side when thinking hard — like they're listening to something inside
- **Typical expression:** A mix of curiosity and hesitation — not frowning, not fully smiling, somewhere in between
- **Clothing:** Comfortable, practical — soft sweatshirts, cozy shoes, a small backpack with a simple star patch

### Personality

- **Core trait:** Cautious by nature — Wren thinks before acting, feels deeply, and notices everything
- **Secondary traits:** Imaginative, caring, perceptive, loyal once comfortable
- **Fear in this story:** New things. Not bad things — just unfamiliar things. The feeling of not knowing what comes next.
- **What makes Wren lovable:** Wren is not dramatic about fear — it's quiet and real. And when Wren does try, the courage is genuinely hard-won and worth celebrating.
- **What makes Wren relatable to ages 4–7:** Every cautious child will see themselves in Wren. The experience of standing at the edge of something new and feeling the "no" feeling before the "yes" feeling — that is universal.

### Voice and Language

- **How Wren speaks:** Short sentences. Honest. Wren says exactly what they mean.
- **Phrases Wren might use:** "I don't know if I can." / "What if it's bad?" / "My tummy feels funny." / "Oh. It wasn't that bad."

### Story Role

- **Where Wren starts:** Safe at home, comfortable with the familiar
- **What changes:** A new thing appears — the specific new thing is TBD based on final concept (could be: first day at a new class, trying a new food, meeting a new child, going to a new place)
- **Where Wren ends:** Having taken one small brave step and discovered that "after" is usually okay — and sometimes even good

## Supporting Character

**Working name:** Bo *(working draft — trademark search required before finalizing)*

**Relationship to Wren:** Slightly older friend, neighbor, or sibling — someone safe

**Role in the story:** Bo doesn't push Wren. Bo doesn't say "just try it." Bo does the thing first, calmly, and lets Wren watch. Bo is patient. The example is the invitation.

**Physical description (brief, original):**
- A little taller than Wren, relaxed posture
- Easy smile — not flashy, just steady
- Warm, open body language — never looming or imposing

**Personality:**
- Calm, patient, quietly encouraging
- Does not make Wren feel bad for being scared
- Is genuinely comfortable with new things — not because they're not scared, but because they've tried many times before

## Visual Character Notes for Illustrator

*(No artist or studio style references. These notes describe original direction only.)*

- Overall visual style: Soft, rounded, watercolor-adjacent. Warm color palette — yellows, soft oranges, peach tones. When Wren is scared, cooler blues appear gently. When Wren is brave, warm colors return.
- Wren's expression should do most of the storytelling — the face is the emotional anchor of each spread
- Bo should always be slightly further into the new situation than Wren — showing the path without pulling
- The "brave brain" concept (if used visually) should feel warm and golden — not mechanical or robotic
- What to avoid: Any visual style that resembles Disney princess aesthetics, Pixar character proportions, or any other known animated franchise look
