## 3. Protagonist Role

**What the protagonist is:**

[Child Protagonist] is an ordinary child, around age 5–7, who feels things deeply. They are not labelled, diagnosed, or defined by their big feelings — they simply experience them with the full intensity that young children do. They are emotionally sensitive without being emotionally unstable. They love the things they love with whole-hearted enthusiasm. When small frustrations arrive — as they do for every child — the feelings build faster than the child expects, and before they know it, the burst is coming.

**What the protagonist is not:**

- Not naughty, bad, broken, or "a difficult child"
- Not defined by their overwhelm — that is one part of one ordinary morning
- Not special in a problem-defining way — they are special in the universal, human way every child is

**Why children will see themselves in this character:**

The child is not dealing with a dramatic crisis. They are dealing with something small — something every 5–7 year old recognises. The universality of the frustration is the point. Any child who has ever felt something building in their body and not known what to do with it will recognise this character immediately.

## 5. Recommended Protagonist Name

> ⚠ **WORKING NAME ONLY — NOT FINAL. Requires Elise approval and manual search checks (Section 11) before use.**

### Gender Direction — Analysis Before Name Selection

The protagonist's gender presentation is a strategic decision for this book, not only a creative one. Three valid directions exist:

**Option A — Gender-neutral protagonist**
- Broadest possible reader identification: any child with big feelings can see themselves
- Illustrator has flexibility to present the character ambiguously or Elise confirms a gender at illustration stage
- Widens institutional/classroom appeal — teachers seek books that work for all students
- Best name candidates: **Noa** (89) or **Ari** (85)
- Trade-off: some parents specifically searching for "boys big feelings books" may not self-select as clearly

**Option B — Boy-coded / soft-boy protagonist**
- Directly addresses the high-intent parent search pattern: parents of boys who shout, cry, struggle to calm down, or melt down are an active, underserved buyer segment in SEL picture books
- Many of the top-selling anger/big feelings books feature girl or gender-neutral characters — a boy protagonist is a genuine shelf differentiator
- Emotional regulation content is still culturally associated as more "for girls" — a boy protagonist sends a direct signal to parents of boys
- Best name candidates: **Eli** (87) or **Teo** (86)
- Trade-off: girl readers and parents may feel slightly less targeted

**Option C — Girl-coded protagonist**
- Strong existing pattern in SEL picture books (many successful books use girl protagonists)
- Wide parent appeal — parents of girls also actively seek emotional regulation titles
- Best name candidates: **Lena** (83) or **Nora** (82)
- Trade-off: does not target the boy-parent underserved segment; less differentiated from existing competition

**Elise's current stated preference:** Gender-neutral or soft-boy, with Noa, Eli, or Teo as top candidates.

## 6. Caring Adult Role

**Function in story:** The caring adult is present at the turning point — the moment before the burst — and helps [Child Protagonist] notice what their body has already been saying. The adult does not rescue the child. They do not fix the frustration. They offer a quiet, calm presence and one simple question or observation that opens the door: *"I can see something is building. Can you feel it too?"*

**Character description:**

- Calm and unhurried in their manner — they are not alarmed by the child's building feeling
- Warm without being effusive — no over-the-top responses
- Non-shaming — they do not say "you shouldn't feel that" or "stop it"
- Simple in language — they use words the child can hold onto
- Patient — they give the child space to respond, not a pre-packaged solution
- Present — they notice the child before the child notices themselves

**Role boundary — the adult is not the hero:**

The regulation tool is used by the child. The adult's role is to help the child notice the signal — not to regulate for them, not to take over, not to become the lesson-deliverer. By the end of the story, the child carries the tool; the adult was the bridge to it.

**Relationship options (Elise to confirm at Gate 10 sign-off or Gate 11):**
- Parent or caregiver at home (widest use case — most read-aloud scenarios)
- Teacher or school counselor (widens classroom and institutional appeal)
- Grandparent or other trusted adult (warm, intergenerational read)

**Working placeholder name for caring adult:** [Caring Adult]
Final name (if any) deferred to Gate 11 or Elise's direction. A caring adult in a picture book often does not need a named reference — many strong SEL picture books keep the adult figure as "Mum," "Dad," "my teacher," or simply unnamed.

## 7. Character Personality

### [Child Protagonist] — Noa (working name — gender presentation TBD at illustration stage)

**Strengths:**
- Genuinely enthusiastic and full of energy when things are going well
- Creative and imaginative — gets absorbed in what they're doing
- Caring toward others — notices when someone else is sad
- Persistent — keeps trying even when things don't work the first time

**Vulnerabilities:**
- Feelings arrive fast and full — they don't yet have a way to notice them before they peak
- When frustrated, they tend to push through and ignore the signals their body is sending
- Small things that go wrong feel very large when they stack on top of each other
- They don't always have words for what they're feeling before it bursts

**Emotional triggers in this story (ordinary, relatable):**
- Something that was working suddenly stops working
- Feeling unheard or unseen in a small way
- A second frustration arriving before the first one has settled
- The gap between what they expected and what actually happened

**Body signals (the visual/sensory language of their feelings building):**
- Hands squeeze tight around whatever they are holding
- Cheeks go warm
- Tummy fills with something heavy and warm
- Breath comes faster and shallower
- Voice rises without meaning it to
- Shoulders lift toward their ears
- The room around them begins to feel "too much" — too loud, too bright, too full

**What [Child Protagonist] wants:**
- To keep doing the thing they love, without interruption
- To be understood without having to explain themselves
- To feel the way they felt at the beginning of the story — before the feeling came

**What [Child Protagonist] learns:**
- Their feelings always send a signal first — they just didn't know to listen for it
- Noticing the signal early gives them a moment before the burst
- Hand on heart + one breath + naming it is something they can actually do
- Feelings are not wrong — they just need to be noticed

**How [Child Protagonist] changes by the end:**
- They don't change completely — they are still the same child with the same feelings
- But they carry one new thing: the knowledge that their body speaks to them before their feelings burst
- The close of the story shows them sitting calmer, with the feeling settled (not gone), and a quiet understanding: *next time, I'll know to listen earlier*

## 8. Visual Direction — High Level Only

*No final illustration prompts in Gate 10. These are directional notes for the character bible only. Illustration prompts are a Gate 11 deliverable.*

**Overall feel:**
Warm, modern, child-centered picture-book illustration style. Rounded forms, approachable faces, expressive body language. Everyday settings — home, bedroom, play space, or classroom. Not fantastical, not clinical. The world looks like a real child's morning.

**Protagonist visual notes:**
- Rounded, expressive face capable of showing subtle shifts in emotion — not just big obvious expressions
- Body posture carries as much emotional information as the face (hunched shoulders, tight grip, rising chest)
- Ordinary, everyday clothing — nothing costume-like or character-branded
- Age reads clearly as around 5–7

**Emotional progression shown through:**
- Body posture (open and relaxed → gradually tightening → very contracted → then releasing)
- Facial expression (happy → mildly confused → trying to ignore something → struggling → noticing → relief)
- Environment (warm and organised → subtly more cluttered/busier → at the peak: overwhelming detail/colour → at the resolution: calmer, stiller)
- Small sensory details (the pencil gripped too tightly, the puzzle piece that won't fit, the sound that suddenly seems too loud)

**What the illustrator must avoid:**
- No separate emotion creatures accompanying the child
- No brain-figure character or brain-setting
- No colored emotion blobs (no Inside Out / Pixar emotion design language)
- No dragon, monster, or external anger-figure
- No control-room or headquarters-in-the-head visual concept
- No "Little Brain / Loud Brain / Brave Brain" branding in any visual element
- No style references to named illustrators

## 9. Body-Signal Visual Language

*These physical signals are the story's emotional vocabulary. They are shown through the child's body and the surrounding environment — not through separate characters or creature-metaphors.*

| Body Signal | What It Looks Like in Illustration | What It Feels Like for the Child |
|---|---|---|
| **Tight hands** | Fingers curled around an object; knuckles visible; grip visibly firmer than needed | "My hands feel squeezed — like they want to hold something very tight" |
| **Warm cheeks** | Cheeks slightly flushed or warm-toned in colour; child may touch face | "My face feels warm and I don't know why" |
| **Full / warm tummy** | Child's hand moves toward belly; body posture curls slightly inward | "Something heavy is sitting in my tummy" |
| **Fast breathing** | Chest rising more visibly; slight parting of lips; stillness replaced by a kind of vibrating quality | "My breath is going fast like after running, but I wasn't running" |
| **Rising voice** | Visual cue: other objects in scene responding (a cup vibrating, a cat's ears flattening); child's expression surprised by their own volume | "My voice got loud and I didn't mean it to" |
| **Shoulders lifting** | Shoulders drawn up toward ears; neck shortening; head pulled slightly forward | "My shoulders went up all by themselves" |
| **Room feeling too much** | Background environment becomes denser, busier, more saturated — visual crowding that resolves at the calm moment | "Everything felt like it was too loud and too close at the same time" |

**Key principle:** At no point do any of these signals become a separate creature, character, or entity. They are what the child feels in their own body, shown through the child's body and the world around them.
