## 3. Global Art Direction

*These rules apply to every spread, every image, every cover option. No exceptions.*

### Style
- Warm modern children's picture-book illustration
- Soft emotional realism — not cartoony-flat, not hyper-detailed
- Rounded, approachable forms
- Cozy, familiar home interior setting
- Child-centered compositions — Noa is always the emotional anchor of the frame
- Highly expressive body language — posture and hands carry as much emotion as the face

### Color Progression (Book-Wide Arc)

| Phase | Pages | Palette guidance |
|---|---|---|
| **Opening — warm and clear** | 5–6 | Bright, airy — morning yellows, soft blues, clean whites. Spacious composition. |
| **First friction** | 7–8 | Mostly the same warmth, but with one slightly dull/muted note — the pale yellow that isn't right. |
| **Building — subtle shift** | 9–12 | Colors begin to feel warmer, slightly closer together. Nothing alarming yet. |
| **Feeling grows** | 13–14 | Amber and warm orange tones entering. Tummy-heaviness reflected in slightly denser composition. |
| **Stacking + burst-approaching** | 15–18 | Most saturated, most crowded. Rich warm reds and deep ambers. Background details multiply. Room begins to feel too full visually. |
| **The catch** | 19–20 | Slight release — one area of calm enters the frame (the grown-up). Saturation begins to ease. |
| **Regulation** | 21–24 | Softer, cooler tones returning — soft blues and creams. Composition opens up. |
| **Settled** | 25–26 | Gentle, warm but not intense. Room is calm again. |
| **Reconnection + closing** | 27–30 | Late-morning warmth — soft gold, quiet cream. Breathing space in the composition. |

### Setting Continuity
- Primary setting: a home interior — Noa's own room or a kitchen/family table
- Craft table or low surface holds the card-making materials throughout
- Same room should be recognisable across spreads — consistent background elements
- Environmental density tracks Noa's emotional state: tidy and spacious at the opening; denser and more cluttered-feeling at the peak; quieter and more settled at the resolution

### Illustration Medium Direction (no artist reference)
- Suggest to illustrator: soft texture consistent with gouache, watercolour, or digital media with painterly quality
- Avoid: hard outlines only (no stark flat vector), photorealistic rendering, or highly graphic/geometric style
- Warm paper tone or light background preferred throughout

**Body-signal visual language for Noa (the book's core visual vocabulary):**

| Signal | How it looks |
|---|---|
| **Tight hands** | Fingers curl around the pencil or card; knuckles slightly visible; grip clearly firmer than needed |

## 5. IP / Safety Negative Prompt Rules

*Every spread prompt and cover option carries these restrictions. They are listed here once and referenced on each spread as "global negative prompts apply."*

**The following must never appear in any image for this book:**

- Separate emotion characters of any kind — no creatures, figures, or entities that represent a feeling
- Colored emotion figures — no color-coded emotional beings (no Inside Out / Pixar emotion cast or any visual resembling it)
- Blob or spot characters — no shapeless emotion creatures of any form
- Dragon, monster, or external anger figure — no fire-breathing anger creature, no rage-beast
- Brain character or brain-as-setting — no brain personification, no "headquarters in the head," no brain control-room
- Any visual design resembling the Pixar Inside Out emotion system, A Little SPOT system, Train Your Angry Dragon, or World of Kids Emotions character systems
- Any character, costume, setting, or visual trait recognisable as Disney, Pixar, DreamWorks, Nickelodeon, PBS, or Sesame Street properties
- Text inside the image unless specifically requested (text belongs in the Canva layout layer, not the illustration layer)
- Logos, watermarks, brand marks of any kind
- Copying or closely resembling the cover or characters of any existing children's picture book identified in Gates 7–8 research
- Named artist style references in any prompt ("in the style of [artist name]" is prohibited)
- Studio style references ("Pixar-style," "Disney-style," "Ghibli-style," etc.)

## 6. Visual Consistency Protocol

*This section governs the required pre-generation steps before any spread images are created. Gate 12 approval authorises prompt direction only — it does not trigger immediate mass-generation of all images. Reference sheets must be created and approved by Elise before any spread image generation begins.*

### Why This Protocol Exists

AI image generation cannot reliably keep the same child, caring adult, room, card, and art style consistent across 13 spreads without approved reference images. If references are skipped, each spread risks producing a visually inconsistent book that cannot be assembled for publication.

### Required Reference Assets — All Must Be Approved Before Spread Generation

#### 1. Noa Character Sheet

Generate a single reference sheet showing Noa in the following views and expressions:

- Front view (full body)
- Three-quarter view
- Side view
- Happy / excited expression (opening joy, spread 1)
- Frustrated / determined expression (push-through, spreads 3–4)
- Almost-burst expression (peak distress, spread 7)
- Hand-on-heart pose (regulation moment, spread 9)
- Calm / settled expression (closing, spread 13)

*Approved Noa character sheet must be used as reference for every spread generation.*

#### 2. Caring Adult / Mom Reference Sheet

Generate a single reference sheet showing the caring adult (mom figure) in the following:

- Front view
- Three-quarter view
- Seated beside Noa (at Noa's level)
- Calm expression — not alarmed, not fixing, simply present
- Hand resting gently (not pointing, not reaching for the card)
- Non-looming posture — adult should never visually dominate the scene

*The adult reads as a consistent mom-like caregiver across all appearances (pp.19–26). Manuscript says "a grown-up" for read-aloud flexibility, but the illustration must show the same woman throughout.*

#### 3. Room / Setting Reference Sheet

Generate a single reference sheet establishing the home interior setting:

- Same home room — consistent walls, floor, and window
- Same craft table or low surface
- Same window with morning light source
- Same pencil cup and craft supplies layout
- Same paper and card materials

*Environmental density changes with Noa's emotional state, but the underlying room should be recognisable as the same space in every spread.*

#### 4. Special Card Reference — Damage Progression

The card must be tracked across its appearances as a single consistent object:

- Blank card (spread 1, pages 5–6)
- Pale thin yellow line on card (spread 2, pages 7–8)
- Blue smudge across the middle (spread 5, pages 13–14)
- Torn card — clear tear across the middle (spread 6, pages 15–16)
- Imperfect-but-still-Noa's card — smudge + small tear + pale yellow visible (spreads 12–13, pages 27–30)

*The card's damage progression is a visual throughline of the story. It must be consistent.*

#### 5. Style Reference Sheet

Generate a single style reference confirming the overall illustration approach:

- Warm modern children's picture book — not a graphic novel, not a comic strip
- Soft emotional realism — not cartoony-flat, not hyper-detailed
- Natural child proportions — no mascot-like proportions, no studio-style resemblance
- Color arc reference swatch: airy bright opening → warm amber buildup → peak saturation (reds/ambers) → calm soft-gold ending

### Spread 1 — Pages 5–6

**Manuscript text:**
> Noa had the best idea. / "I'm going to make a card," said Noa. / "The most beautiful card anyone has ever seen." / Pencils out. Paper ready. Every color waiting. / Noa could hardly sit still.

**Emotional beat:** Pure opening joy — Noa full of excitement and possibility. This is the before. Everything is good. The reader should feel Noa's fizzy, barely-contained enthusiasm.

**Visual composition:**
Wide, airy spread. Noa is seated at a low table or on the floor, leaning forward eagerly into a spread of craft supplies — pencils, paper, small cups of color. The composition is open and spacious. Plenty of negative space. The card itself is a fresh blank sheet of paper in front of Noa. Noa's arms are spread wide or reaching toward supplies — a gesture of energy and readiness.

**Body-signal cues:** None yet. Noa's body is open, relaxed, forward-leaning with excitement. This is Noa at their best.

**Color/mood:** Brightest, most airy spread in the book. Morning light through a window. Warm yellows and clear blues. Clean, tidy room. Pencil colors are vivid and welcoming. The palette is at its most spacious and inviting here.

**Character consistency:** Establish Noa's full character design here — this is the reader's first clear sight of Noa. Face should be fully legible and joyful. Body language is expansive, not contracted.

**Text placement:** Text can sit in upper portion of spread or alongside Noa — Canva layout will determine; leave clear negative space in at least one-third of the composition for text overlay.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. Additionally: no sad expressions, no tension, no environmental crowding.

### Spread 2 — Pages 7–8

**Manuscript text:**
> But the yellow was almost gone. / Noa picked it up anyway / and pressed it to the paper. / The line came out thin. / Pale. / Not yellow enough. / Not the bright, bold yellow Noa needed at all.

**Emotional beat:** First tiny frustration — something small has gone wrong. Not catastrophic yet. A flicker. The feeling has begun but Noa doesn't know it yet.

**Visual composition:**
Medium shot — Noa at the table, holding a worn-down yellow pencil stub (visibly almost finished). Noa has pressed it to the card and is looking at the result — a pale, thin yellow line against the paper. Noa's expression shows a small "oh no" — eyebrows slightly furrowed, mouth uncertain — but Noa is still trying. The card is visible with the disappointing pale yellow mark on it.

**Body-signal cues:** Early and subtle — Noa's brow has a small crease. The grip on the pencil is normal but just slightly firm. Nothing dramatic yet. The reader notices a small flicker before Noa does.

**Color/mood:** Still warm and bright overall, but one note is slightly off — the yellow in the image is pale and thin where it should be bold. That tonal mismatch IS the visual tension of this spread.

**Character consistency:** Same Noa as spread 1, same clothing, same setting. Expression has shifted just slightly — joyful → uncertain.

**Text placement:** Card and pencil should be in foreground; Noa slightly back. Space for text alongside or below Noa.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. No dramatic distress — this is a tiny, quiet problem.

### Spread 3 — Pages 9–10

**Manuscript text:**
> "It's fine," said Noa. / "It's fine. It's fine." / Noa pressed harder. / But their hand was gripping the pencil / a little tighter than before.

**Emotional beat:** The push-through. Noa is trying to override the frustration. "It's fine." The body is quietly disagreeing — the grip is tighter now, even though Noa doesn't notice.

**Visual composition:**
Noa pressing the pencil harder to the card — a visible physical effort. The expression is determined, set, slightly tense — brow still furrowed but mouth closed in determination. Noa is still trying to make the card work. The hand gripping the pencil is visibly tighter than it needs to be — this is the first body-signal the reader can clearly see, even though Noa cannot.

**Body-signal cues:** **Tight hands** — the first body signal. Pencil gripped firmly, knuckles starting to show. Noa's posture is leaning in harder than before — a subtle physical push that mirrors the emotional effort of "it's fine."

**Color/mood:** Same warm setting, but the mood has shifted one notch. The light feels the same but Noa's patch of the frame is slightly warmer — the heat of effort and suppression is beginning.

**Character consistency:** Same Noa, same setting. Small but clear shift in body posture from spread 1 (open, joyful) to this spread (determined, slightly contracted). Clothing and hair the same.

**Text placement:** The hand and pencil should be clearly visible — they are the emotional story on this page. Leave space for the brief text alongside or above Noa.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. No external anger figures. Body signal must belong to Noa's own body — not an external symbol.

### Spread 4 — Pages 11–12

**Manuscript text:**
> Tighter. / And tighter. / Noa didn't notice.

**Emotional beat:** A visual beat — sparse text, the image carries everything. The reader sees what Noa cannot. The grip is too tight. No one has said so yet.

**Visual composition:**
This spread is a close-up or enlarged view — the primary subject is Noa's hand around the pencil. Fingers wrapped too tightly, knuckles slightly white. In the background or secondary frame, Noa's face is visible but turned toward the card — fully absorbed in drawing, completely unaware of what the hand is doing. The contrast between Noa's oblivious expression and the clearly-too-tight grip is the emotional story of this spread.

Composition option A: Full-spread close-up of the hand, Noa's face small in the background.
Composition option B: Split or layered composition — large hand in foreground, Noa's full figure in softer focus behind.

**Body-signal cues:** **Tight hands** — this is the primary and central visual. Must be clear and specific — not a cartoon fist, but a child's hand gripping something harder than they need to.

**Color/mood:** The hand itself can be slightly warmer-toned than the rest of the spread — a subtle visual signal. The background remains warm but unchanged. The contrast is quiet, not dramatic.

**Character consistency:** Noa's face (even in background) must remain recognisably the same Noa. The hand must also be recognisably child-sized and belong to this character.

**Text placement:** Sparse text ("Tighter. / And tighter. / Noa didn't notice.") — can float in white space alongside the hand image. Canva layout will determine exact placement.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. The hand is Noa's body signal — not a symbol, not a creature, not a metaphor object. No external annotation or label overlays on the image.

### Spread 5 — Pages 13–14

**Manuscript text:**
> Then the blue smudged — right across the middle. / Something warm and heavy / settled into Noa's tummy. / Breath coming a little faster. / A little faster.

**Emotional beat:** Second setback — the feeling inside Noa is growing. The body is responding more clearly now: tummy warmth, faster breath. Noa is still at the table but something has visibly shifted in the body.

**Visual composition:**
Noa is looking at the card, which now has a smear of blue across carefully drawn lines — the smudge is visible and central. Noa's face registers dismay — not a full meltdown yet, but clearly something is wrong. The body is also speaking now: Noa's free hand has drifted toward their belly (tummy signal); the chest is slightly more raised (faster breath). Shoulders are beginning to rise — just a little.

**Body-signal cues:** **Warm tummy** (hand drifting toward belly, body curving slightly inward) + **faster breath** (chest more raised, lips very slightly parted). These are the first two internal signals made visually external through Noa's body, not through any external creature.

**Color/mood:** The warm tones are intensifying — ambers and oranges beginning to enter the palette around Noa. The smudge on the card is visually striking — a grey-blue sweep across what should have been a careful drawing. The room looks the same but Noa's body occupies the frame differently: more contracted, more full.

**Character consistency:** Same Noa, same room. Clothing unchanged. Face now registers real feeling — not just "oh no" (spread 2) but something heavier.

**Text placement:** The smudged card should be clearly visible. Noa's body position (hand near belly) should be readable. Space for the longer text on the facing page or alongside the image.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. The "warm and heavy" feeling in Noa's tummy is NOT a creature, not a blob, not a color-coded object inside Noa's body. It is shown only through Noa's body posture and expression.

### Spread 6 — Pages 15–16

**Manuscript text:**
> And then — rrrip. / The paper tore. / "NO!" said Noa — / louder than they meant to. / Much louder.

**Emotional beat:** The stacking moment — a second frustration on top of the first. The paper tears. Noa's voice comes out louder than they intended. This is the most dramatic external moment so far — but the burst hasn't happened yet.

**Visual composition:**
The card is torn — a clear tear across the middle, visually central to the spread. Noa's face shows shock and distress — mouth open, eyes wide, the expression of "that was too loud" — surprised by the volume of their own voice. Something in the environment reacts to the sound: a small pet's ears flatten, or a pencil cup rattles, or a bird outside the window pauses mid-motion. The world registered the sound too — this reaction shot makes the loudness visible.

**Body-signal cues:** **Rising voice** — shown through the environmental reaction (pet, cup, bird) and through the surprise on Noa's own face. Noa did not mean to be that loud, and they know it. Shoulders are now clearly raised toward the ears.

**Color/mood:** A sharp, high-energy moment — the most visually active spread so far. Warm, saturated. The torn card is the focal point. The environmental reaction adds movement and energy to the frame.

**Character consistency:** Noa is clearly distressed but not fully erupted. The expression should read "surprised by my own reaction" — not pure rage. Same Noa, recognisable face, same setting.

**Text placement:** The torn card and Noa's expression are the key visual elements. The "NO!" could be suggested by the environmental reaction rather than text overlay. Text block for the story words sits alongside or below. No text inside the illustration itself.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. The loud voice is shown through environmental reaction and Noa's own expression — not through a separate anger creature or external symbol.

### Spread 7 — Pages 17–18

**Manuscript text:**
> Everything felt too full. / Shoulders up near the ears. / Cheeks hot. / Hands squeezing tight. / The card felt ruined. / The whole morning felt ruined. / Something was about to burst.

**Emotional beat:** Pre-burst peak — the most intense spread in the book. All body signals are present simultaneously. The burst feels imminent. The reader and child can both feel it.

**Visual composition:**
This is the fullest, most visually intense spread. Noa's entire body is contracted: shoulders high near the ears, hands clenched tight, cheeks visibly flushed warm, chest full. Noa's face holds maximum distress — not yet broken into tears or yelling, but right at the edge. The environment around Noa has become denser — background elements are more numerous, more saturated, more crowded, as if the room itself has grown too full. The ruined card is on the table. The visual weight of the spread should feel almost too much — intentionally crowded and warm.

**Body-signal cues:** All body signals present simultaneously:
- **Shoulders up** — very visible, near the ears
- **Cheeks hot** — warm flush, clearly visible
- **Hands clenched** — fists or tightly gripped objects
- **Full chest** — breath held, body contracted
- **Room too full** — background visual density at maximum

**Color/mood:** Peak saturation of the book. Warm reds, deep ambers, concentrated oranges. The room feels heavy and hot. This spread should feel like the reader needs a breath too.

**Character consistency:** Same Noa — face fully recognisable even in maximum distress. Clothing the same. This is Noa at their most overwhelmed, not a different character.

**Text placement:** The spread can have text overlaid on a slightly less dense area (perhaps upper portion or facing page). The image itself should breathe even in its density — leave one visual resting point.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. All body signals belong to Noa's body only. No external creature represents the "about to burst" feeling. No literal explosion imagery.

### Spread 8 — Pages 19–20

**Manuscript text:**
> A grown-up came in / and sat down beside Noa. / Quietly. / Not to fix it. / Not to say anything yet. / Then: / "I think something is building in your body," / said the grown-up. / And — all at once — so could Noa.

**Emotional beat:** The catch — the grown-up arrives and names what Noa couldn't. This is the turning point. The room begins to breathe again. The grown-up's presence is the first signal that the burst will not come.

**Visual composition:**
The grown-up enters and sits beside Noa — at Noa's level (sitting on the floor or crouching beside the table, not standing over Noa). The grown-up's face is calm, warm, unhurried. They are looking at Noa, not at the card. Noa is still contracted — shoulders up, expression tight — but there is the very beginning of something shifting. The grown-up's calm energy is visible in their posture and expression. The room has very slightly more breathing room than the previous spread — the density is beginning to ease.

**Body-signal cues:** Noa still shows most signals from spread 7 — but the presence of the calm grown-up creates a counterweight. The grown-up's body is open, relaxed, close but not crowding.

**Color/mood:** Slight easing of the intensity. The warm saturation of the previous spread begins to settle — slightly cooler note entering the palette with the grown-up's presence. Not dramatic — subtle.

**Character consistency:** Grown-up introduced here for the first time. Must establish their visual identity clearly — warm adult, calm face, at Noa's level. This appearance should match across spreads 19–26.

**Text placement:** The grown-up's face and Noa's face should both be visible and readable. The dialogue ("I think something is building in your body") could be suggested by their positions — text floats alongside the scene.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. The grown-up does not fix anything in this spread — no reaching for the card, no pointing, no correction gesture. Their entire body says "I am here, I see you."

### Spread 9 — Pages 21–22

**Manuscript text:**
> "Can you feel it?" the grown-up asked. / Noa put a hand on their heart. / Ba-dump. / Ba-dump. / Yes. / Noa could feel it.

**Emotional beat:** Hand on heart — the regulation tool begins. Noa turns inward for the first time. This is the quietest moment so far. The room begins to truly settle.

**Visual composition:**
Noa's hand rests on their own chest — the gesture is small, tentative, then stiller. Noa's eyes are softer, beginning to turn inward. The grown-up is close but not touching — giving Noa the space to feel this themselves. The grown-up's expression is gentle encouragement. The room around them is beginning to breathe — less dense, colours settling. This spread should feel quieter than anything that has come before in the second half of the book.

**Body-signal cues:** The hand-on-heart gesture is the primary visual — it must be clear, central, and quiet. This is NOT a dramatic gesture — it is small and real. Noa's face is beginning to soften. Shoulders have come down slightly from their peak position.

**Color/mood:** Softer blues and creams returning. The warm amber of the crisis spreads begins to ease. The composition opens up — less visual crowding.

**Character consistency:** Same Noa, same grown-up from spread 8. Noa's face is transitioning — from maximum distress (spread 7) toward something quieter. This is the midpoint of that transition.

**Text placement:** The hand-on-heart gesture must be fully visible. Text can float above or alongside — this spread has quiet composition that invites the text to breathe too.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. The heartbeat ("Ba-dump") is Noa feeling their own heartbeat — it is NOT represented as an external sound, a visual wave, a creature, or a symbol. If illustrated, the heartbeat is implied through Noa's expression and the hand position only.

### Spread 10 — Pages 23–24

**Manuscript text:**
> One slow breath in. / One slow breath out. / "I feel..." Noa said. / "I feel like I might burst." / The room felt a little quieter.

**Emotional beat:** One breath + naming the feeling — the emotional climax of the regulation sequence. Noa speaks the feeling out loud for the first time. The naming is the release. The room gets quieter.

**Visual composition:**
This is the stillest spread in the book. Noa's eyes are partly closed — mid-breath, chest rising then falling in one visible, slow breath. The grown-up is present but slightly back — giving Noa the space to do this themselves. Noa's face holds the beginning of something softer — not relief yet, but the edge of it. The ruined card is visible on the table in the background — still there, still imperfect, but no longer the entire world. The room is calm.

**Body-signal cues:** **Breath** — the primary visual signal. This is the one slow breath, shown through Noa's body: chest gently raised, eyes softened, body a fraction less contracted. The breath is the pause.

**Color/mood:** The softest, most settled palette of the second half of the book. Soft blues, creams, and warm whites. The room has de-escalated visually. This spread should feel like the exhale it describes.

**Character consistency:** Noa at the emotional turning point. Same character, but clearly transitioning. The grown-up is still present — same visual identity as spreads 8–9.

**Text placement:** The composition should have a natural resting point — perhaps upper area or facing page — for the brief, powerful text. The image's stillness should complement the text's stillness.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. Noa names the feeling with words — the feeling is not represented as an external creature, blob, or character at the moment of naming. The feeling is in Noa's body and Noa's words.

### Spread 11 — Pages 25–26

**Manuscript text:**
> The feeling didn't go away. / But it didn't burst, either. / Noa and the grown-up sat together / for a little while. / For now, that was enough.

**Emotional beat:** The feeling settles — not disappears. Noa and the grown-up simply sit together. This is co-regulation: presence, not fixing. The burst did not happen.

**Visual composition:**
Noa and the grown-up sitting together — side by side or facing each other slightly — in a simple, quiet posture. Not doing anything in particular. Just there. The card is visible in the background, still smudged and torn — but Noa is not looking at it right now. Noa's body is clearly more open than the crisis spreads: shoulders lower, hands unclenched, face quieter. The grown-up is warm and still beside them.

**Body-signal cues:** Noa's body signals are beginning to resolve: shoulders lower, hands open or resting loosely, face less tight. The feeling is still present — this should not read as "all better" — but Noa is no longer at the edge of the burst.

**Color/mood:** Gentle, warm, settled palette. Late-morning quality of light — warm but no longer intense. The room is calm and spacious again.

**Character consistency:** Same Noa, same grown-up. Both are present and visible. The grown-up is beside Noa — not in front of, not looming over.

**Text placement:** The quiet, shared moment should be central. Text floats alongside in the space the composition provides. This spread has room to breathe.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. The "feeling" that didn't go away is NOT represented as a character, creature, or external object. It is simply present in Noa's not-quite-fully-relaxed body.

### Spread 12 — Pages 27–28

**Emotional beat:** Reconnection with the object of frustration — from a different place. The card is still imperfect. But Noa is different. This is quiet acceptance, not forced positivity.

**Visual composition:**
Noa is looking at the card again — sitting at the table or crouching near it. The card is visible with its imperfections: the smudge, the small tear, the pale yellow. Noa's expression is quieter and steadier — looking at the card with something like curiosity or gentle acceptance, not distress. Perhaps Noa's hand reaches toward the card — not yet touching or fixing, just considering. The grown-up may be in the background or out of frame — Noa is beginning to hold this moment on their own.

**Body-signal cues:** Body signals are largely resolved — shoulders at normal height, hands unclenched. The feeling is present only as a very low background hum. Noa's posture is open again — not as wide-open as the very first spread, but settled and steady.

**Color/mood:** Warm, quiet gold — late morning. Not as bright as the opening (something has happened and Noa knows it) but warm and accepting. The imperfect card is part of the scene, not the crisis.

**Character consistency:** Same Noa. Face should read as recognisably the same child who began the story — but with something new in the expression. Thoughtful, quiet, steady.

**Text placement:** Noa and the card should both be clearly visible. Text can sit alongside or on the facing page.

**Negative prompts:** Global negative prompts apply. No magical "fixing" of the card. The card's imperfections remain visible. No external emotion figure accompanying Noa's acceptance.

## 8. Cover Direction Brief

*Three cover concept options. These are direction briefs only — not final cover prompts. No cover image is generated until Gate 12 is signed and Elise selects an option.*

---

### Option A — Noa with the Imperfect Card

**Concept:** Noa holding the card — smudged, slightly torn, pale yellow — with a calm, steady expression. Not beaming with joy, not distressed. The quiet acceptance of "it's still mine." The card's imperfection is visible; Noa's calm is the story.

**Cover hook:** "The feeling doesn't have to be perfect to be okay."

**Parent buyer appeal:** Signals immediately that this is not a "just calm down" book — the imperfection is accepted, not erased. Parent recognises the realistic resolution.

**Child appeal:** A child holding something they made — relatable, warm. The slightly imperfect card invites a child reader who knows the feeling of a creation not going right.

**IP risk:** Low. Original composition, no franchise resemblance. The card is incidental; the child and their expression are the story.

**Negative prompts:** No emotion creatures visible. No external anger figure. No brain character. Card is simply a card — not a metaphor object with a face.

---

### Option B — Hand on Heart *(Recommended)*

**Concept:** Noa seated at or near the craft table, one hand on their heart, expression calm and grounded. Warm light. Craft supplies softly present in the background. The card may be partially visible. Noa looks out or slightly upward — quiet, steady.

**Cover hook:** "Before the burst, there's a moment. This is it."

**Parent buyer appeal:** The hand-on-heart gesture is immediately recognisable as a self-regulation tool — parents searching for SEL picture books will recognise the positioning signal. The warm, calm image projects a positive outcome: this book works.

**Child appeal:** A child in a familiar setting, doing something small and important — not dramatic, not scary. Warmth, not lecture.

**IP risk:** Low. The hand-on-heart gesture is not owned by any franchise. Original composition, original character.

**Why recommended:** This option most clearly signals the book's positioning — upstream emotional regulation tool, warmth, child agency, positive outcome. It is the most searchable image (parent scanning Amazon thumbnails will read "calm child, self-regulation, feelings") and the most differentiated from existing anger/big-feelings book covers that show peak emotion.

**Negative prompts:** No emotion creatures. No brain character. No external anger figure. No "burst" imagery on the cover — the cover shows the moment of regulation, not the pre-burst.

---

### Option C — Body Signal Glow (Abstract Option)

**Concept:** Noa's silhouette or partial figure, with soft warm shading or subtle colour areas indicating the body signal locations — hands, chest/heart, tummy, cheeks — rendered as warm, gentle warmth, non-magical and non-superpower-like. Not clinical, not medical. The shading reads as "these are the places your feelings live."

**Cover hook:** "Your body always tells you first."

**Parent buyer appeal:** Visually distinctive — nothing else in the SEL picture book space uses this approach for the cover. Immediately communicates "body awareness" to parents familiar with somatic or OT-based approaches.

**Child appeal:** Potentially less immediately inviting than Options A or B — the abstraction may read as "adult" to a browsing child. Works better for the institutional buyer (teacher, counselor).

**IP risk:** Low, but requires careful execution — the soft body-signal shading concept must not resemble any existing franchise character system (e.g., must not resemble a "life force" or "power" visual from any superhero franchise). Keep the shading warm and gentle, non-magical and non-superpower-like, not dramatic.

**Why not top recommendation:** Higher design execution risk; lower immediate child appeal for the primary buyer (parent shopping for a child). Better as an internal-spread visual than a cover if Option B is available.

---

**Cover recommended option: Option B — Hand on Heart.**

## 9. Back Matter Visuals — Pages 31–32

*Visual direction only — no Canva layout, no final image generation. These are suggestions for the design stage.*

**Purpose of back matter:** Parent-facing. Simple and warm. Not a workbook — no heavy visual complexity.

### Suggested visual elements

**Body outline for "My Feelings Signal Map":**
A simple, friendly child body silhouette — gender-neutral, rounded, approachable. Not clinical (no anatomical detail). Child should resemble Noa loosely but function as a generic "draw on this" template. Small enough to fit within the back matter page space alongside the checklist and parent phrases.

**Small icons for the body signals checklist:**
Six simple spot icons, one per signal. Each icon is a small, clear illustration:
- Hands squeezing → simple closed hand or gripping gesture
- Tummy warm or heavy → simple belly/tummy area with subtle warm body-signal shading
- Breath faster → small chest with slight rise or gentle wave lines suggesting breath
- Voice louder → small open mouth or sound wave (very subtle — not aggressive)
- Face hot → simple face with warm cheeks
- Shoulders up → simple shoulders drawn toward ears

Icons should be warm-toned, rounded, and child-friendly. Not clinical. Not frightening. Each should feel like a friendly clue, not a warning sign.

**Simple parent-child calm moment (optional spot illustration):**
A small spot illustration of an adult and child sitting together — the adult at the child's level, both calm. Minimal detail — two figures, quiet warmth. Could anchor the parent phrases section. Optional; Elise to confirm whether to include at design stage.

**What to avoid in back matter:**
- No workbook-heavy design
- No multiple activity columns
- No clinical body diagrams
- No emotion character illustrations anywhere in the back matter
- No "before and after" anger transformation imagery
