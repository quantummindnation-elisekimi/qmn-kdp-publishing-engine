## Review Mining Notes

*From Elise's review mining of Breathe Like a Bear (top competitor) and The Story of Little Heart and Brave Brain (nearest concept competitor).*

**What parents praise most:**
Breathe Like a Bear reviews center on two things: the exercises work in real-world moments (car, grocery store, bedtime, meltdown onset), and children actually use them. Parents describe their children learning specific techniques by name and requesting them in high-stress moments. The book is recommended by behavioral therapists and OTs by name, giving it professional credibility. Teachers implement exercises in classrooms with success. Parents of trauma-background and sensory-sensitive children specifically call it out as genuinely effective.

**What parents wish was different:**
Reading between the lines of Breathe Like a Bear reviews: parents describe the book primarily as a TOOL — not as a story their child loves. Reviews rarely say "my child begged to read it again." The exercises are the product. The gap this implies: parents of overwhelmed children are also looking for a narrative that creates calm by being read — a story a child is emotionally drawn to, not only a technique collection they turn to during a crisis.

**Language parents use to describe the problem:**
"can't calm down," "dysregulated," "sensory-sensitive," "trauma background," "meltdown," "overwhelmed," "needs to regulate," "behavioral therapist recommended." One reviewer describes a child who "works himself up until he can't even talk" — the physical escalation that parents can see but can't interrupt. Teachers reference using the exercises with "students who need a moment."

**Unmet needs mentioned in reviews:**
No narrative picture storybook for ages 4–7 specifically about finding calm appeared in the search results. The gap is a storybook — one a child returns to not because it is a behavioral tool but because it tells a story about a child who discovers what calm feels like and how to reach for it. The exercise format (Breathe Like a Bear) dominates the current market; the story format is absent.

## Market Gap Hypothesis

**Primary gap identified:** No children's narrative picture storybook exists for ages 3–7 about finding calm. The search phrase returns 114 results dominated by adult books and one dominant children's exercise book (Breathe Like a Bear). The narrative storybook format — a child protagonist who experiences overwhelm and learns what calm feels like, told as a story worth requesting at bedtime — is genuinely absent.

**Secondary gap:** Breathe Like a Bear occupies the "calm-down technique" shelf. No book occupies the "calm as an emotional state worth naming and recognizing" shelf. The distinction matters: Breathe Like a Bear tells a child what to do. A QMN storybook would tell a child what calm feels like — creating emotional recognition, not only technique recall.

**Key competitive note:** Breathe Like a Bear is a Top-100 Amazon seller (BSR #9,571) with Rodale Kids backing, 6,310 reviews, and professional recommendations from behavioral therapists and OTs. Any QMN entry must be clearly positioned as a storybook (not an exercise collection) to avoid direct format competition. The story format is the differentiation, not just better execution of the same format.

**Validation still needed:** Elise should also search: `calm down storybook kids`, `finding calm picture book ages 4-7`, `calm brain children's book`, `self-regulation story kids ages 3-7`. These phrases would test whether the narrative storybook gap holds across multiple entry points.

## Copyright / IP Risk Notes

**Lower IP risk than The Little Loud Brain niche.**

**Inside Out (Disney/Pixar) — LOWER risk than Loud Brain.** "Calm" does not map directly to a named Inside Out character in the way that "anger" or "brain loudness" might. Inside Out's Bing Bong and Bing Bong 2 don't occupy the "finding calm" emotional space. Risk remains that any brain-character imagery would conflict with Inside Out's general brain-control-room concept — apply the same guardrails as Loud Brain: child as protagonist, no personified emotions, no control-room setting.

**"The Story of Little Heart and Brave Brain" (Nov 2025) — PROXIMITY FLAG for QMN Book 001.** This self-published book uses "Brave Brain" as a character name. It currently has only 2 reviews and minimal traction, but Elise should:
1. Confirm QMN has a USPTO application or intention for "The Little Brave Brain" title

**Breathe Like a Bear:** No IP risk. The exercise-format and Kira Willey brand are distinct from QMN's narrative storybook concept.
